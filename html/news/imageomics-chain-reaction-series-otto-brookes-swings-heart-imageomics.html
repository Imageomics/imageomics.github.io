Imageomics Chain Reaction Series: Otto Brookes Swings into the Heart of Imageomics

 

Chain Reaction: A new spotlight series where one rising researcher lights the path to the next. Follow the chain, meet the minds shaping the future of Imageomics.

When colleague Dominik Winecki described Otto Brookes' work as "pure Imageomics," he highlighted a research endeavor epitomizing the fusion of biology and machine learning. Brookes, a PhD candidate at the University of Bristol, is at the forefront of developing AI systems that extract meaningful behavioral traits from images and videos of wild apes, directly supporting the Imageomics Institute mission.

Brookes' academic journey began with a degree in Biomedical Science from the University of Warwick, followed by a master’s degree in computer science at the University of Bristol. This interdisciplinary foundation paved the way for his current research focusing on automating the detection and identification of great apes in both zoo and wild environments. His early work included developing a deep learning pipeline for facial recognition of individual gorillas at Bristol Zoo Gardens, achieving impressive accuracy rates.

Central to Brookes' current research is the PanAf20K dataset—the largest and most diverse open-access video resource of wild chimpanzees and gorillas. Utilizing this dataset, he has developed a triple-stream deep metric learning framework that integrates RGB appearance, motion, and pose data to generate embeddings reflecting behavioral similarity in a biologically interpretable manner. This approach aligns with ethograms and captures trait variation relevant to ecological and evolutionary analysis.

In collaboration with researchers, Brookes introduced ChimpVLM, a vision-language model that embeds expert-derived ethograms into the learning process, linking visual observations with semantic biological knowledge and improving recognition performance. His most recent work examines how behavior is entangled with environmental context, quantifying the extent to which background habitat features influence trait recognition.

Brookes' passion for science was nurtured from a young age by his engineer parents, who encouraged curiosity and problem-solving. Inspirational science teachers further fueled his interest, leading him to explore the intersection of biology and computer science.

"Imageomics is a natural fit for me—it combines both fields to answer biological questions with computational tools," he said.
