Imageomics Chain Reaction Series: Luke Meyers' Spotlight

Luke Meyers, a master’s student in computer science at the University of Puerto Rico, is using artificial intelligence to study something small, but critically important: pollinators. And his work is stirring up the hive in ecological circles.

Meyers’ latest project, which applies computer vision to track ecological change in a Hawaiian cloud forest, was recently accepted for oral presentation at the 5th International Workshop on Camera Traps, AI, and Ecology (CamTrapAI 2025), taking place September 9–10 in Seattle.

His paper, "Tracking Phenological Status and Ecological Interactions in a Hawaiian Cloud Forest Understory using Low-Cost Camera Traps and Visual Foundation Models," explores how AI and affordable field technology can work together to monitor plant cycles and native bird behavior in complex environments.

“We were able to directly measure flowering, fruiting, and leafing using low-resolution camera traps—and also track which bird species were visiting those plants,” Meyers said.

By leveraging large, pre-trained vision models, Meyers and collaborators were able to avoid the need for time-consuming data labeling. The approach opens the door for ecological monitoring in places and communities that previously lacked access to high-cost tools.

Originally from California and fluent in both English and Spanish, Meyers describes himself as an “interpreter between disciplines, cultures, and languages.”

His research combines pollinator ecology, sustainable agriculture, and explainable AI to develop accessible monitoring tools across the neotropics. His thesis centers on building a “foundation model for honeybees,” using millions of images from hive footage to re-identify individual bees and enable behavioral research at a scale never before possible.

Meyers is also an active member of the Imageomics Institute community. At FuncaPalooza 2025, he tested BioCLIP2 tools for extracting functional traits, collaborated on pollinator and fish morphology projects, and appreciated the chance to connect across disciplines.

“It was awesome. I met so many amazing people and gained a better understanding of how Imageomics tools can work with real data,” he said.

Continuing the Chain Reaction spotlight series, Meyers nominates Valentin Gabeff, a researcher at the École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne (EPFL) who conducts research primarily in deep learning applications for wildlife monitoring and epileptic seizure detection.
