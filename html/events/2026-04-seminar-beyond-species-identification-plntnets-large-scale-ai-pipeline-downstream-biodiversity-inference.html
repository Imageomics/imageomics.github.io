Please join us on Thursday April 9 @ 10am ET for an Imageomics Seminar, Beyond Species Identification: Pl@ntNet’s Large-Scale AI Pipeline for Downstream Biodiversity Inference, with Dr. Pierre Bonnet.

Abstract: Pl@ntNet platform has evolved from a popular plant identification tool into a massive, AI-driven engine for computational ecology. This talk explores the core design and architecture of Pl@ntNet, detailing how millions of crowdsourced botanical images are processed to accurately identify species globally. Beyond taxonomic identification, we will examine how this rich data stream produces novel ecological insights through cutting-edge downstream applications. Highlights include GeoPl@ntNet's species distribution modeling based on high-resolution satellite and environmental data, as well as our diverse approaches to analyzing plant communities, focusing on the implementation of Large languages models (cf. Pl@ntBERT) or multi-scale tiling frameworks for complex plant image analysis. Finally, I will discuss the recent opening of the platform for automated plant disease identification, and explore the potential of Pl@ntNet's spatio-temporal data for conducting large-scale ecological studies.

Speaker Bio: Pierre Bonnet is a biologist at CIRAD and co-lead of the Pl@ntNet citizen science platform. His research sits at the interface of ecology, biodiversity informatics, and machine learning, with a focus on designing and evaluating AI methods for plant identification and on translating large, heterogeneous biodiversity data streams into operational monitoring services. His work spans methodological contributions (from data curation and model evaluation to large-scale mapping workflows) and the deployment of end-user tools that enable practitioners to explore, validate, and use biodiversity indicators derived from major data infrastructures. He is the coordinator of the EU-funded GUARDEN project, and involved in the MAMBO EU project, dedicated to the development of new methods for large scale biodiversity monitoring and evidence-based decision-making. He also serves as a representative of Pl@ntNet within the World Flora Online consortium, supporting interoperability between citizen-science observations, taxonomic reference systems, and global flora initiatives.
