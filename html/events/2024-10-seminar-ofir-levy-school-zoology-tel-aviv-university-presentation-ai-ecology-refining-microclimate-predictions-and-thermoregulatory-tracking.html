Title:

"AI in Ecology: Refining Microclimate Predictions and Thermoregulatory Tracking"

Abstract:

Climate conditions that animals experience are a major force determining their survival, life-history, activity patterns, and fitness. To calculate these forces and estimate the impacts of environmental change, we must deeply understand the microclimates they experience (i.e., climate conditions in their proximity) and how these conditions influence their lives. Traditional methods of measuring microclimates involve labor-intensive fieldwork, and existing microclimate models often need refinement to accurately capture the true conditions animals face. Traditional thermoregulation tracking methods can be observational or invasive, potentially disturbing natural behaviors. In the era of big data and AI advancements, we can enhance our understanding of microclimates and non-invasively monitor thermoregulatory behaviors. Using drone data and a spatial heat-balance microclimate model, we demonstrate that a random-forest model can predict and correct microclimate model errors, highlighting overlooked parameters in microclimate calculations. Additionally, we reveal behavioral thermoregulation patterns using camera-trap data combined with computer vision AI and microclimate measurements. This approach enables continuous tracking of animal microhabitat preferences under varying conditions. Our findings suggest that AI models can bolster our capacity to understand the thermal environment and animal behavior, thereby informing conservation strategies in our changing climate.

Bio:

Ofir Levy is a Senior Lecturer at the School of Zoology at Tel Aviv University since 2018. He earned his Ph.D. from Tel Aviv University's Department of Zoology in 2010, where his research focused on understanding how ecological, physiological, and evolutionary forces shape organismal activity patterns. Following this doctoral work, he completed a postdoctoral appointment at the School of Life Sciences at Arizona State University in 2017. He has utilized field studies and computer simulations ever since, to explore how habitat loss and climate change affect animal performance and survival. More recently, he expanded his research to include the use of artificial intelligence to gain a deeper understanding of the environment and animal responses to various climatic conditions. This includes developing AI-driven tools for identifying animals’ responses to climate through camera traps and creating high-resolution microclimate mapping to support biodiversity research and management.
