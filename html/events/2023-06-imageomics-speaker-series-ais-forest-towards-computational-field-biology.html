Tilo Burghardt, UG Programme Director at the Dept of Computer Science at Bristol University, UK.

In 1963, the British conservationist Sir Peter Scott and his family started re-identifying individual Bewick's swans from hand drawings of unique beak patterns during the species' annual migratory visit to the Slimbridge wetlands near Bristol. This study still runs today and represents one of the earliest and longest standing individual animal identification projects in the world. Over the last 60 years digital technology, communication networks, and now artificial intelligence have transformed such gathering and interpretation of ecologically relevant data for field biology and firmly placed measuring manifestations of life (i.e. bio-metrics) in the computational realm. This talk will trace some of the key concepts at the heart of this evolving subject cluster - from earliest ideas to current AI-driven systems. It will try to relate emerging modern terminology such as imageomics, animal biometrics, and ecological computer science within the wider context of computation and biology. Finally, the talk will try to depict examples how today's 'third revolution' in visual computational biology is being applied to conservation, ecology, and even farming, and how neighbouring disciplines such as robotics, genetics, remote sensing, behavioural sciences, and taxonomics can contribute towards evolving the subject. Various lines of work at the University of Bristol will serve as examples to show the diversity of the emerging interdisciplinary science. The talk will highlight some key technical challenges today and call for a renewed focus on cross-disciplinary science.

Bio: Dr. Tilo Burghardt is a computer science academic who researches animal biometrics and computer vision methods for the life sciences. His work focuses on using artificial intelligence and robotics to support biodiversity, sustainability, and animal welfare. His interdisciplinary research links computing with ecology, conservation, taxonomics, healthcare, animal husbandry, and sustainable farming. He was one of the first researchers to monitor animals in their natural habitat via automated real-time computer vision methods in the early 2000s. Over the past two decades he made contributions to establishing animal biometrics as an emerging cross-discipline linked to imageomics and routed in pattern recognition and artificial intelligence. Tilo's enthusiasm for computer science and computer vision is reflected in his dedication to teaching the subject. He has received the University of Bristol 'Award for Education' for his educational contributions to the Engineering Faculty.
