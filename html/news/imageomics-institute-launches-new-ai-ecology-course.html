June 25, 2024

Imageomics Institute Launches New AI-Ecology Course

A new course developed by the Imageomics Institute is set to bridge the fields of artificial intelligence (AI) and ecology.

The course, “Experiential Introduction to AI and Ecology,” aims to revolutionize ecological research and conservation efforts through interdisciplinary collaboration and next-generation training. 

Supported by the U.S. National Science Foundation and the Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council of Canada, the primary goal is to foster interdisciplinary scientific inquiry at the intersection of AI and ecology. Key collaborators include the National Ecological Observatory Network (NEON), the AI and Biodiversity Change (ABC) Global Climate Center, the Hawaii Division of Forestry and Wildlife, and multiple universities such as the University of Hawaii and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

Imageomics researchers traveled to Hawaii in May to begin field studies, meet with potential collaborators, outline projects and to set up the course foundation.

Imageomics Institute Director Tanya Berger-Wolf said by bringing together biologists, computer scientists, and experts from related disciplines, the year-long program seeks to enhance understanding and address pressing questions in ecology, biodiversity, and conservation.

She said the newly-launched course has evolved from initial AI/Ecology classes taught over the years, as well as the previous Experiential Imageomics Course.

“This collaboration is a vital step toward integrating AI in ecological research, fostering a new generation of researchers dedicated to preserving our natural world,” Berger-Wolf said.

The course is structured into three parts:

  1. An initial virtual segment covering foundational skills.
  2. A three-week field experience in Hawaii for data collection.
  3. A concluding project-based phase where students analyze their data.

Initial collaborations include the American Bird Conservancy, ASU's School of Ocean Futures, and Hawaii Volcanoes National Park, among others. Projects will explore topics such as predicting bird song variations, characterizing mosquito populations, and developing tools to combat Rapid Ohia Death, a fungal disease affecting Hawaiian trees.

NEON Chief Scientist and Observatory Director Paula Mabee joined the network collaboration and outreach efforts in Hawaii.

“We at NEON are excited to help host the Imageomics and ABC students and provide the infrastructure needed to make them successful,” she said. “This is an excellent opportunity for the students to use NEON data where relevant to their projects, and in turn, we are looking for ways to ensure NEON data are ready to be used in research involving AI and machine learning.”

Next Steps

Enrollment for the course begins six weeks prior to the start date, with the field experience scheduled for January. Participants will engage in both virtual and hands-on learning, contributing to projects that address real-world ecological challenges.
