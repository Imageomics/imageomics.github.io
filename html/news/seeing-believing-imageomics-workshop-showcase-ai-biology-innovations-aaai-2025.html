November 13, 2024

Seeing Is Believing: Imageomics Workshop to Showcase AI-Biology Innovations at AAAI 2025

Researchers from The Ohio State University’s Imageomics Institute will showcase their Second Workshop during the annual 2025 AAAI Conference focused on bridging artificial intelligence and biological science.

The AAAI is dedicated to advancing the scientific understanding of the mechanisms underlying thought and intelligent behavior and their embodiment in machines. Its annual conference series aims to promote research in AI and foster scientific exchange between researchers, practitioners, scientists, students, and engineers across the entirety of AI and its affiliated disciplines. 

Set for March 3 in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, the Imageomics Second Workshop will explore ways AI can help scientists “see” new biological traits through image analysis. Paper submissions remain open until November 24.

Imageomics—a new field emerging through the NSF-funded Imageomics Institute—uses images, from microscopic cell views to videos of wildlife, to draw out meaningful biological data such as morphology, behavior, and even genetic insights. By developing computational methods to reveal anomalies human biologists may not be able to detect, researchers are creating tools that extend beyond human sight, advancing both AI and biological science.

The Ohio State-led organizing committee, which includes Dr. Wei-Lun (Harry) Chao, Dr. Jianyang Gu, Dr. Yu Su, and Imageomics Director Dr. Tanya Berger-Wolf, will host the one-day workshop. With support from experts at Virginia Tech, Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, and the University of Bristol, the team aims to deepen interdisciplinary connections within this growing field.

Research topics welcomed include AI techniques to identify biological patterns from image data, translating machine learning insights back into biological research, and addressing broad scientific challenges through collaboration. 

“This workshop will be an invaluable opportunity for AI and biology researchers to join forces,” said Dr. Chao. “By integrating biological knowledge into AI models, we can achieve breakthroughs that go far beyond our current understanding.”

Organizers encourage biologists, AI researchers, and those interested in interdisciplinary studies to join and participate. The workshop will tackle key questions, such as how to blend data from biology with AI methodologies and how foundation models for vision and language can benefit from biological insights.

Those interested in shaping the future of Imageomics may submit their work through the official submission portal by November 24 at 11:59 p.m.
