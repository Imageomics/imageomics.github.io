Predicting multiple dimensions of plant diversity using airborne imagery and hierarchical models

Tracking biodiversity across biomes over time has emerged as an imperative in unified global efforts to manage our living planet for a sustainable future for humanity. We harness the National Ecological Observatory Network (NEON) to develop routines using airborne imaging spectroscopy to predict multiple dimensions of plant diversity at a continental scale across biomes in the US. Our findings indicate that diversity metrics based on spectral species (SS) from airborne spectroscopy data show strong and positive associations with ground-based species richness and other dimensions of plant diversity. In contrast, metrics based on distance matrices did not. Using a multi-level modeling framework, we find that spectral diversity consistently predicts analogous metrics of plant taxonomic, functional, and phylogenetic dimensions of biodiversity across biomes. The approach demonstrates promise for monitoring dimensions of plant diversity by integrating ground-based measures of biodiversity with imaging spectroscopy and advances capacity towards a Global Biodiversity Observing System.
